## SECTION 6 — COMPETITOR ANALYSIS

### Overview

Competitor risk is a 5-point penalty dimension in the scoring model. The AI must assess competitive risk based on: which competitor is mentioned, what stage of evaluation has occurred with the competitor, and whether the competitor has structural advantages in the specific customer's decision matrix.

**Competitive Risk Tiers:**

| Tier | Definition | Score Impact |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| MILD | Customer mentioned competitor casually; no active evaluation | -1 to -2 |
| MEDIUM | Customer is actively comparing; test drive or quotation with competitor | -3 to -5 |
| HIGH | Customer has strong preference for competitor or has booking with competitor | -6 to -10 |

---

### 6.1 Hyundai (Creta, Verna, i20, Venue, Alcazar, Ioniq 5)

**Brand Profile:** Hyundai is the most formidable competitor in the Indian mass-premium segment. The Creta is the top-selling compact SUV and the single most common competitive threat in dealership sales environments. Hyundai has an exceptional service network, strong residual values, and deep brand recognition.

**Mild Risk Scenario:**
Customer mentioned Creta in passing while asking about your Nexon or Brezza. Has not visited Hyundai yet.
- Score adjustment: -1
- Recommended response: Proactively compare safety ratings (if your model has an edge), highlight unique features, offer a side-by-side comparison sheet.

**Medium Risk Scenario:**
Customer has visited a Hyundai dealership and received a Creta quotation. Is now comparing final numbers between the two.
- Score adjustment: -4
- Recommended response: Identify the one thing that makes your car better for this specific customer (family size, safety, tech, running cost). Offer test drive on the same day. Match or explain the value gap.

**High Risk Scenario:**
Customer visited Hyundai twice, said "the Creta feels more premium," and is asking you for a price match as a final check.
- Score adjustment: -7
- Recommended response: Escalate to sales manager immediately. Offer highest available discount + benefits. Emphasise brand-specific advantages (warranty, ground clearance, safety rating). If the deal cannot be saved commercially, acknowledge it gracefully — preserve the relationship for future.

**Key Hyundai Advantages to Acknowledge and Counter:**
- Brand perception (counter: newer safety standards, better warranty)
- Service network (counter: certified multi-brand service options)
- Resale value (counter: verify segment-specific data, do not assume)

### 6.2 Kia (Seltos, Carens, Sonet, EV6)

**Brand Profile:** Kia is a premium positioning competitor with strong technology perception, excellent build quality, and growing service network. The Seltos competes directly against Creta and your relevant products. Kia buyers tend to be aspirational, digitally aware, and tech-forward.

**Mild Risk Scenario:**
Customer browsed Kia website, mentioned "Seltos looks good" but hasn't visited.
- Score adjustment: -1
- Counter: Highlight your model's value-for-money advantage, lower running cost, or better segment-specific features.

**Medium Risk Scenario:**
Customer test drove Seltos, impressed with interior quality, now comparing with your top variant.
- Score adjustment: -4
- Counter: Address the specific feature that impressed them (interior? tech? ride quality?) with a focused counter-argument. Offer premium accessories to close the perceived gap.

**High Risk Scenario:**
Customer's shortlist is Seltos vs. your model. Spouse prefers Seltos. Customer is asking for matching the Kia finance deal.
- Score adjustment: -8
- Counter: Involve finance manager. Offer total cost of ownership comparison. Bring in service cost, insurance premium difference, and real-world fuel economy data.

### 6.3 Tata Motors (Nexon, Harrier, Safari, Punch, Altroz, Curvv)

**Brand Profile:** Tata has achieved a dramatic brand transformation since 2020 through best-in-class NCAP safety ratings. The Nexon EV is a category leader in entry-level EVs. Safety messaging is Tata's primary competitive weapon.

**Mild Risk Scenario:**
Customer mentioned Nexon EV as an option but hasn't visited Tata dealership.
- Score adjustment: -1
- Counter: Compare total cost of ownership, charging infrastructure limitations in their area, service network comparison.

**Medium Risk Scenario:**
Customer specifically mentioned 5-star safety rating of Nexon as a concern about your model.
- Score adjustment: -4
- Counter: Do NOT dismiss safety concern — validate it and address it with your model's safety data. Mishandling safety objection causes immediate trust loss.

**High Risk Scenario:**
Customer has received Nexon EV quotation, spouse is strongly advocating for EV to save on petrol.
- Score adjustment: -7
- Counter: Total cost of ownership calculator showing break-even period. Charging infrastructure reality check for their specific location. Home charging cost analysis.

### 6.4 Mahindra (XUV700, Scorpio-N, Thar, XUV300, BE 6)

**Brand Profile:** Mahindra is a threat in the SUV and lifestyle segments, with the XUV700 in particular offering exceptional features at a relatively competitive price. The Thar/Scorpio are strong brand lifestyle plays. Mahindra's waiting period for popular models can be a competitor weakness.

**Mild Risk Scenario:**
Customer is considering XUV700 but is deterred by the long waiting period.
- Score adjustment: -1 (their weakness = your opportunity)
- Counter: Highlight your availability versus Mahindra's 6–12 month waiting period for XUV700.

**Medium Risk Scenario:**
Customer test drove Scorpio-N, impressed by the road presence and off-road capability.
- Score adjustment: -4
- Counter: If you're selling a similar SUV — compare urban drivability, boot space, ride quality on-road. Lifestyle benefit comparison.

**High Risk Scenario:**
Customer has confirmed XUV700 booking and is now asking for exchange valuation from your dealership only.
- Score adjustment: -9
- The lead is effectively dead for this transaction. Mark as DEAD and maintain goodwill for future business.

**Medium Risk Scenario:**

## SECTION 7 — BUDGET ANALYSIS

### Overview

Financial readiness is a 15-point scoring dimension and one of the most critical determinants of conversion probability. The AI must assess not just whether a customer can pay, but when they can pay and with what confidence. Budget analysis requires both declared signals and inferred signals.

---

### 7.1 Ready to Buy — Own Funds

**Definition:** Customer has liquid funds (savings, FD maturity, proceeds from sale) available to fund the purchase without any external dependency.

**Score:** 13–15/15

**Indicators:**
- Customer mentions savings, fixed deposit, or investment return
- Customer asks about exact on-road price to confirm cash readiness
- Has brought cheque/DD to showroom
- No mention of EMI or loan despite asking for final price

**Example:**
> Rajesh, 48, retired government employee. Received ₹20 lakh from PF settlement. Looking for a car in the ₹12–15 lakh range. Visited showroom with his son, asked for on-road price, said "we'll pay cash."
> **Budget Score: 15/15** — No financial dependency, funds confirmed, price within range.

**AI Reasoning:** When a customer confirms own funds and the price is within their stated range, financial readiness is maximal. The AI should flag this as a closing opportunity and alert the salesperson immediately.

---

### 7.2 EMI Dependent

**Definition:** Customer intends to finance the vehicle through a monthly installment loan. Financial readiness depends on loan approval and EMI affordability.

**Score:** 7–11/15 (based on employment stability, loan history, and income verification)

**Sub-scenarios:**

**7.2a Salaried with Stable Employment**
- Score: 10–11/15
- Customer has a salary slip, stable employer, and history of EMI repayment
- Example: Meena, 32, software engineer. Monthly salary ₹85,000. Looking at a ₹14 lakh car. Asked for 7-year loan calculation. Has existing home loan being repaid.
- AI note: Existing loan is not a disqualifier if combined EMI is below 40% of income.

**7.2b Self-Employed with Irregular Income**
- Score: 6–8/15
- Higher risk due to income variability; banks may require additional documentation
- Example: Siva, 38, textile shop owner. Monthly income varies ₹40,000–₹1.2 lakh. Wants ₹18 lakh car on EMI.
- AI note: Self-employment income requires ITR verification. Bank approval is uncertain. Flag as medium financial risk.

**7.2c First-Time Loan Applicant**
- Score: 7–9/15
- No prior loan history means no credit score; NBFC route may be needed with slightly higher interest
- Example: Pooja, 24, freshly employed. First job, ₹45,000 salary. Wants Nexon on EMI.
- AI note: Low credit history = processing time risk. Pre-screen through bank before raising customer expectations.

**7.2d EMI Affordability Check:**
As a rule of thumb, the AI should check whether the proposed EMI falls within the following range:
- Safe zone: EMI < 25% of take-home monthly income
- Caution zone: EMI between 25–40% of take-home
- Risk zone: EMI > 40% of take-home

---

### 7.3 Loan Pre-Approved

**Definition:** Customer has received formal loan approval from a bank or NBFC before visiting the dealership.

**Score:** 13/15

**Why This Is High:** Pre-approval eliminates the primary financial uncertainty. The customer knows their eligibility, interest rate, and EMI range. They are shopping with a confirmed budget.

**Example:**
> Karthik, 35, IT manager. HDFC Bank pre-approved ₹16 lakh auto loan at 8.5% for 5 years. Visited showroom with loan approval letter. Asked about cars in ₹16–19 lakh range (₹3 lakh own funds).
> **Budget Score: 13/15** — Pre-approval confirmed, only variant selection pending.

**AI Reasoning:** Pre-approved customers should be escalated to the finance manager for confirmation of terms and immediate stock check for their approved budget range. Time to close is typically 24–72 hours once a pre-approved customer visits.

---

### 7.4 Loan Rejected

**Definition:** Customer's loan application was rejected by one or more banks.

**Score:** 2–4/15

**Scoring by Rejection Stage:**
- Rejected by one bank, other options not yet tried: 4/15 — still recoverable
- Rejected by two banks: 3/15 — high risk, alternative funding needed
- Rejected by all major banks + NBFCs: 2/15 — functionally ineligible for finance

**Example:**
> Mani, 29, delivery executive. Applied for ₹8 lakh loan. Rejected by SBI (insufficient income) and Axis Bank (existing default). Looking at a ₹7 lakh car.
> **Budget Score: 2/15** — Both bank rejections are documented. NBFC at high interest may be the only option; customer may not afford the higher EMI.

**AI Reasoning:** Loan rejection is a serious financial disqualifier. Before reducing score permanently, verify whether NBFC options have been explored, whether the customer has a co-applicant, and whether a lower-priced model resolves the eligibility issue. If all options are exhausted, mark financial readiness score as 2 and reduce overall score accordingly.

---

### 7.5 Budget Mismatch

**Definition:** The customer's stated budget is materially lower than the on-road price of the vehicle they want.

**Score:** 3–6/15 (depending on gap size)

**Gap-Based Scoring:**
- Gap < 10% of vehicle price: 7/15 — manageable; accessories could be adjusted or small loan used
- Gap 10–25%: 5/15 — significant; requires either product downgrade or financial planning
- Gap > 25%: 3/15 — product recommendation must be changed to a lower segment

**Example:**
> Vimal, 42, teacher. Budget: ₹8 lakh. Wants: Grand Vitara (on-road ₹17 lakh). Gap: ₹9 lakh (53%).
> **Budget Score: 3/15** — Massive mismatch. Salesperson must reposition to Brezza or WagonR. Customer's expectations are not aligned with their financial position.

**AI Recommendation:** When budget mismatch is detected, the AI should automatically suggest alternative models within the customer's budget range and generate a comparison showing what they can get versus what they want.

---

### 7.6 Waiting for Bonus

**Definition:** Customer has an anticipated bonus payment that they intend to use as down payment or full payment, but the bonus has not yet been received.

**Score:** 6–9/15 (based on bonus certainty)

**Sub-scenarios:**
- Bonus confirmed by employer, specific date given: 9/15 — high reliability, set a follow-up for bonus date
- Bonus expected but not confirmed: 7/15 — moderate reliability; some employment sectors have consistent bonus patterns
- Bonus mentioned casually with no specifics: 5/15 — low reliability; treat as aspirational

**Example:**
> Sheela, 34, bank manager. Annual bonus of ₹1.5 lakh expected in March. Wants to use it as down payment for a ₹13 lakh car. Currently browsing, timeline is March.
> **Budget Score: 8/15** — Bonus is institutionally reliable (bank HR cycle), specific month given, reasonable down payment requirement. Follow up in February.

**AI Reasoning:** The AI should automatically set a follow-up trigger 30 days before the expected bonus date and re-score the lead at that point. Bonus-dependent customers often convert quickly once the payment arrives.

---

### 7.7 Waiting for Salary Increment

**Definition:** Customer is waiting for a salary increase before feeling comfortable committing to a car EMI.

**Score:** 5–7/15

**Assessment Logic:**
- If current salary supports EMI without increment: score 7/15 — increment is a confidence trigger, not a financial requirement
- If current salary does not support EMI without increment: score 5/15 — genuine financial dependency; conversion requires income event
- If increment is speculative ("I'm expecting a promotion"): score 5/15 — uncertain; treat as 3-month dormant lead

**Example:**
> Dinesh, 27, junior developer. Current salary ₹40,000/month. Wants a ₹12 lakh car; EMI would be ₹22,000. Says increment in Q2 will take salary to ₹55,000. Asked to be contacted in Q2.
> **Budget Score: 6/15** — Current income makes EMI stressful (55% of take-home). Post-increment, EMI drops to 40% — still high but manageable. Set Q2 follow-up trigger.

**AI Reasoning:** Salary increment leads should be placed in an automated nurture sequence with a reactivation trigger at the stated increment date. They are NOT dead leads — they are time-shifted opportunities with defined activation dates.
